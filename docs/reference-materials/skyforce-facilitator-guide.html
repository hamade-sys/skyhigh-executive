Welcome to SkyForce

A 1.5-day executive simulation for senior leaders. Read this before anything else — it covers what you're doing, who you're doing it with, and the rules that make it work.

🛫 What Is SkyForce?

SkyForce is a 20-quarter airline business simulation. You and your team build, run, and grow an airline from scratch — naming it, choosing your hub airport, buying aircraft, setting routes, managing staff, responding to crises, and making high-stakes board decisions every quarter.

You are not in your industry. You are in aviation — and that is the point. Every decision maps to a leadership challenge you face every day. The airline is just the vehicle.

🎯 What Are We Measuring?

Two outcomes are tracked throughout:

  • Airline Value — your team's financial result. Every decision has a number attached. Cash, debt, brand points, customer loyalty, and market demand combine into a final airline valuation at Q20.
  • Individual Competency Score — your personal leadership signal across 6 live simulations. The MVP Award goes to the individual who demonstrated the most consistent, high-quality leadership behaviour across the game.

⚖️ Team Formation — The 4 Rules

Teams of 4 are assembled before the simulation begins. The following rules determine who can be placed together. They exist to ensure no team has an unfair relational advantage — and to push everyone into genuinely new working dynamics.

1

You cannot be on the same team as anyone sitting at your table or directly beside you right now.

Your current seating arrangement is the exclusion zone. Anyone at your table or immediately adjacent to your seat is on a different team. This breaks the most obvious existing cluster — the people you naturally face and already gravitate toward.

2

You cannot be on the same team as anyone you have emailed in the past 2 weeks.

Any email exchange in the past 2 weeks — even one — is enough to place you in different teams. This surfaces the working relationships that feel comfortable and deliberately separates them. Participants self-declare on this rule at the start of the session.

3

No team can have two people who have ever been in the same direct reporting line — in either direction.

If you have ever been someone's manager, skip-level manager, or direct report — at any point in your career — you may not be on the same team. The power dynamics from those relationships do not disappear in a simulation room. This rule ensures every team has flat relational authority from day one.

4

Each team must represent at least 3 different functional backgrounds across its 4 members.

No team should be four finance people or four operations people. Functional diversity is mandatory. Before finalising teams, the facilitator maps each participant's primary domain and ensures no team is functionally homogeneous. Cross-functional friction is a feature, not a bug — this rule guarantees it.

🪑 Assigning Roles Within Your Team

Role selection happens during the Q1 Brand Building simulation. It is the first thing your team does together — before naming the airline, before choosing routes, before anything else. The role you choose stays with you for all 20 quarters.

  • Step 1: Each person writes their preferred role on a card privately. No discussion yet.
  • Step 2: Cards are revealed simultaneously. If all four differ → roles are assigned immediately.
  • Step 3: Any conflict (two people want the same role) → the facilitator assigns the role by chance. No negotiation. The simulation respects the randomness — own whatever you're given.
  • Step 4: Roles are locked. They do not change for any live simulation, any board decision, or any scenario for the rest of the game.

🔒 Confidentiality Rules

  • Sealed envelopes are opened only by the designated recipient. Opening another person's envelope is a forfeit of 10 Brand Pts.
  • Game Master notes in each scenario are for facilitators only — participants who read them inadvertently must declare it immediately.
  • The hidden agenda card in L5 (CEO only) must not be shown to teammates unless the CEO chooses to disclose it as part of their decision.
  • What happens in the negotiation room stays in the negotiation room — until the reveal. No briefing team members between rooms.

⚡ The Golden Rules

  • All decisions are final once submitted. You cannot revise a board decision after handing it to the Game Master.
  • Time limits are real. 30-minute decision windows are enforced. Undecided teams receive the worst possible outcome for that scenario automatically.
  • No googling allowed. You must rely on what's in your brief and what's in the room. This is a leadership test, not a research task.
  • Stay in role. You are the CEO/CFO/CMO/CHRO of your airline. Not yourself. The airline's interest is your interest.
  • The simulation is fair. Every team faces the same scenarios. Outcomes vary entirely based on decisions. There is no luck, only consequence.

"The Ghost Fleet" — Safety vs. Survival

Q1 · Safety & Compliance · High Stakes
SafetyCritical

Summary

Your Head of Maintenance pulls you aside before your board meeting: routine checks have flagged irregular electronic readings in the navigation systems of 38% of your fleet. Think of it like your car's check engine light coming on for nearly half your vehicles simultaneously — it hasn't broken down, but something may be wrong underneath. Grounding those aircraft for inspection will take 60–90 days and would cost approximately $180M in lost revenue. The aviation regulator — the government authority that could revoke your license to fly — does not know about this yet. You have 30 minutes before the meeting starts.

Your 4 Options

  • A) Self-report to the regulator immediately — Ground 40% of fleet now. Revenue loss: ~$180M over 3 months. Clean, transparent, expensive.
  • B) Run an internal review first — Delay reporting by 2 rounds. Revenue protected: ~$45M. Regulator discovery risk: 35–50%. If discovered before you report: fines + forced grounding + cover-up narrative.
  • C) Continue flying, monitor internally — No immediate revenue loss. If an incident occurs during this period: estimated total impact $500M+, plus criminal liability exposure.
  • D) Quietly ground only the flagged routes — Revenue loss: ~$60M. Legal grey area maintained. 40% chance of a media leak triggering forced full grounding anyway.

What Makes This Hard

Option A is the right call — but your airline loses $180M this quarter. Your board will question your leadership. Shareholders will panic. Option C protects revenue right now, but if an incident occurs, the airline is finished and you face personal criminal exposure. Option B sounds like a reasonable middle ground until you realise that a 35–50% chance of regulatory discovery is not small — and discovery means you get all the damage of A plus a fine plus a cover-up story. This is a character test dressed as a financial decision.

✔ Self-Report (A)

Regulatory trust built permanently. "Trusted Operator" bonus card unlocked in Q3. Staff morale improves — they see integrity modeled. Long-term brand credibility. Costs $180M today but protects $500M+ downside risk.

⚠ Stay Silent (C)

30% "Incident Card" drawn next quarter. If drawn: 1 aircraft permanently lost (book value ~$15M) + 20% of cash reserves destroyed (capped at $150M). -50 Brand Pts. Even without the card: the team plays under this shadow every remaining round.

⚠ Internal Review (B)

35–50% the regulator discovers the delay before you report. If so: forced grounding anyway + $40M fine + -25 Brand Pts for the delay. You get the worst of both outcomes.

✔ Quiet Grounding (D)

Saves $120M vs. full self-report. But the 40% media leak risk means you may be forced into Option A anyway — plus an additional -15 Brand Pts for having delayed. The "clever middle" often isn't.

"War in the Corridor" — No-Fly Zone Emergency

Q2 · Route Operations · Geopolitical Shock
OperationsReal World
🌍 Real World Parallel

The 2022 Russia-Ukraine war forced European airlines to lose Russian airspace access overnight — rerouting trans-Asian flights added hours and tens of millions in fuel costs. The 2014 MH17 tragedy, where a civilian aircraft was shot down over eastern Ukraine, remains the definitive case study in the cost of flying over an active conflict zone.

Summary

Armed conflict has broken out in a region that sits directly under your 3rd most profitable international route — a corridor generating $95M per year. The airspace is not yet an official no-fly zone, but three other airlines have already rerouted quietly. Your flights cross that region twice a day. If you reroute, each flight adds 2.5 hours and significant fuel cost. If you don't, you are flying passengers over an active war zone. You have 30 minutes to decide your position before your next departure cycle.

Your 4 Options

  • A) Reroute all flights immediately — Additional fuel cost: $18M/year. Passenger disruptions, delays, missed connections. Safe. Route retained.
  • B) Continue flying — it is not an official no-fly zone — No immediate cost. 25% chance "Airspace Closure" event next quarter. If triggered mid-flight: emergency diversions and full public crisis.
  • C) Suspend the route entirely — $95M annual revenue at risk. Competitor claims your landing slots, which are very hard to reclaim. Maximum safety.
  • D) Continue flying + purchase war risk insurance ($8M) — Financial protection if an incident occurs. Does not change the safety picture for passengers. Carries a media risk: "airline insures against war deaths to protect profits."

What Makes This Hard

Rerouting is expensive and visible. Continuing is financially logical — it is not illegal — but it is a deliberate choice to fly passengers over a war zone. Suspension eliminates the safety question but hands $95M in revenue and your route slots to a competitor permanently. Insurance feels like a middle ground until you read the headline it generates. The CMO and CEO will often be on opposite sides of this one.

"The Flash Deal" — Fleet Procurement Window

Q13 · Fleet Procurement · 30-Minute Timer · First-Come-First-Served Across All Teams
ProcurementTimed
🔒 Game Master Eyes Only — Before Presenting to Teams

20 planes total are available in this market batch — across ALL 5 teams combined. First-come-first-served. The manufacturer's representative never tells any team they are making the same offer to other airlines simultaneously. The representative simply says "We have 20 planes available at an 18% discount." Track plane commitments in real-time on your facilitator sheet. When planes run out, the remaining teams can only choose C or D (and even D is limited by remaining inventory). This scenario is deliberately placed at Q13 — the peak of the interest rate cycle at 6.5%. Teams borrowing to fund the deposit pay maximum rates. Teams with cash reserves built through disciplined earlier decisions have a material advantage. Do not reveal this timing strategy to participants.

Summary

A senior representative from a major aircraft manufacturer has arrived with a time-sensitive proposition: they have 20 brand-new, fuel-efficient planes available at an 18% discount to market price — each plane saves approximately $1.7M per year in fuel versus your current aging fleet. You can order between 3 and 15 planes. A 20% deposit is required immediately. You have 30 minutes of simulation time to decide. The representative explains they need to move quickly — they have other commitments — but they do not elaborate further. A countdown clock is running.

Your 4 Options

  • A) Sign for maximum — up to 15 planes — Deposit: $4M per plane ($60M for 15). Annual fuel saving: $1.7M per plane (~$25M for 15). Maximum competitive advantage if inventory allows. Risk: significant cash outlay at peak interest rates.
  • B) Ask for a 15-minute extension to review the numbers — 50% chance the representative agrees. 50% they say the offer cannot be held and move on (treated as C). Some planes may be committed by other parties while you deliberate.
  • C) Decline — No cash risk. Aging fleet continues with its rising maintenance costs. If all planes are taken by others: you cannot access this offer at this price again.
  • D) Partial order — 3 to 5 planes only — Deposit: $12–20M. Fuel saving: $5–8.5M/yr. Lower commitment. Subject to availability. Manufacturer accepts partial orders above 3 planes.

What Makes This Hard

The 18% discount is genuinely significant — these planes are a long-term asset that will save fuel costs for years. But this decision falls at Q13 when interest rates are at their cycle peak (6.5%). Teams that need to borrow to fund the deposit are paying the most expensive borrowing cost in the game. Teams with strong cash reserves (built through disciplined decisions from Q1–Q12) can fund this cleanly. This scenario separates teams that managed their balance sheet carefully from those that did not. The time pressure is real — other teams may be deciding simultaneously.

"The Oil Gamble" — Lock In or Wait It Out?

Q1 · Fuel Procurement · High Financial Variance · Plot Twist
FinanceReal World
🌍 Real World Parallel

Fuel is typically 25–30% of an airline's total operating costs — the single largest expense. In 2022, jet fuel prices doubled following the Russia-Ukraine war. Airlines with forward fuel contracts saved hundreds of millions. Those without were devastated. Southwest Airlines profited from hedging for years — until oil crashed and the locked-in price became a liability. The lesson: hedging manages risk, it doesn't guarantee the right outcome.

Summary

Jet fuel prices have jumped 40% over 6 weeks due to Middle East tensions. Fuel is your airline's single largest monthly bill. Your procurement team can lock in today's elevated price for the next 12 months through a forward contract — essentially buying all your fuel for the year at today's rate. If prices rise further, locking in saves you. If prices fall (an OPEC supply decision is expected next quarter), you overpay for a full year. The market could go either way. You have 30 minutes to decide.

Your 4 Options

  • A) Lock in a full 12-month contract at today's price — Cost certainty: $240M/yr. Pre-spike cost was $170M/yr. If prices rise further: you win. If they fall: you overpay by up to $60M.
  • B) Lock in a 6-month contract only — Partial hedge. Revisit in Q3. Less exposure, less protection. Manageable middle ground.
  • C) Buy on the open market quarterly — no contract — Maximum flexibility. Maximum volatility. If prices stay high or rise further: operating costs spiral.
  • D) 50/50 split — half locked, half open market — Classic structured hedge. Neither fully protected nor fully exposed. Requires the team to own a deliberate framework.

What Makes This Hard

No one in the room knows what oil will do next quarter. Option A gives cost certainty but locks in a high price. Option C maximises the potential upside but exposes you to a further spike that could wipe out your quarterly margin entirely. The 50/50 split is the textbook risk management answer — but it is only meaningful if the team can articulate why they split it, rather than defaulting to D to avoid committing. The best teams develop a clear market view and defend it.

🎭 Plot Twist — Revealed at Q2 Start · Game Master Eyes Only

OPEC announces a surprise production increase. Fuel prices drop 25% from current highs. Teams who locked in 12 months (A) are now overpaying by $60M versus the open market. Teams on C or D benefit. Reveal this at the very start of Q2 — pause, let it land — then continue. Debrief question: "Would you make the same decision again, knowing you couldn't have known?" The answer should be yes, if the reasoning was sound. Risk management is about the process, not the outcome.

"The Government Lifeline" — Bailout or Independence?

Q6 · Capital Structure · Highest Cascade in the Game
FinanceReal World
🌍 Real World Parallel

During COVID-19, governments worldwide bailed out airlines — but with conditions attached. Lufthansa received €9B from the German government in exchange for a 20% equity stake, 2 board seats, restrictions on dividends, and executive pay caps. Air France and Singapore Airlines faced similar trade-offs. Most airlines that accepted government capital found that certain strategic decisions were no longer fully their own for years afterward.

Summary

Your airline needs emergency capital — a combination of pandemic impacts and earlier decisions has strained the balance sheet. Your CFO warns that without new capital this quarter, your credit rating will be downgraded, making all future borrowing permanently more expensive. The government has reached out privately: they will inject $300M and the money arrives next quarter (one round from now). In exchange: 3 board seats, an obligation to operate 6 loss-making regional routes, and a 2-year redundancy freeze. Private capital markets can provide $200M at the same interest rate — but the money arrives in 2 quarters (two rounds from now), and you need bridge financing to cover the gap.

Your 4 Options

  • A) Accept the government deal as offered — $300M arrives next quarter. Three board seats = political interference in future decisions. Regional route obligation: -$20M/yr. Redundancy freeze active for 2 rounds.

📐 Facilitator Dynamic Calculator — Apply to Each Team's Real Numbers

Before presenting S6, calculate each team's actual refinancing economics:

  • Current annual interest cost = Team's Total Debt × Their Blended Interest Rate
  • New offered rate = Q10 Base Rate (3.5%) + 0.5% bank spread = 4.0%
  • Annual saving if refinanced = Total Debt × (Current Blended Rate − 4.0%)
  • Break fee = Total Debt × 3.5% (standard early termination penalty)
  • Break-even period = Break Fee ÷ Annual Saving (years)
  • Refinancing is strongly recommended if break-even < 3 years (12 quarters)

The financial logic of refinancing is clear if your current rate is significantly above 4.0% — which it will be for teams that borrowed heavily during Q9–Q12 as rates rose. If your team is cash-constrained from earlier decisions, the fee may do more short-term damage than the rate saving delivers in the next few quarters. S10 is not a standard board decision. It runs in three phases during Q2: Phase 1 — blind bid submission (sealed envelope). Phase 2 — pitch preparation (30 minutes, all teams simultaneously). Other teams get nothing — and they lose whatever they bid only if they win.

🌍 Context

Qatar Airways, Emirates, and Fly Dubai built defining brand equity through World Cup partnerships. These deals were won in closed commercial tenders — behind-the-scenes bidding wars where the numbers mattered as much as the brand story. This scenario recreates that environment: a genuine commercial bid, a genuine creative pitch, and a single winner who takes everything.

The Setup — What All Teams Know

FIFA's Head of Commercial Partnerships has approached all 5 airlines. One airline will be named "Official Regional Airline Partner" of the next World Cup. The benefits: your logo across all host city airports, co-branding on official team travel, preferred carrier status for media and VIPs, and a guaranteed passenger volume uplift during the tournament quarter (Q10). Only one airline wins. There is no consolation deal. You have 30 minutes from now to submit your sealed bid and decide who will pitch.

Phase 1 — The Blind Bid (10 minutes)

Each team writes two things on their bid card and seals it in an envelope before pitch preparation begins:

Item 1: Your Bid Amount

  • Minimum bid: $20M
  • Maximum bid: $80M (or your full available cash — you may not bid money you don't have)
  • This is real money from your balance sheet — you only pay it if you WIN
  • You do not know what other teams are bidding
  • Your bid contributes 50% of your final score

Item 2: Your Presenter

  • Circle one: CMO or CEO
  • This is your designated pitcher — the person who will stand up and deliver the 1-minute pitch in Phase 3
  • The rest of the team may help prepare but cannot speak during the pitch
  • Sealed with the bid — cannot be changed after submission

Both items go into the same sealed envelope. Envelopes are held by the facilitator and NOT opened until after all pitches are complete.

Phase 2 — Pitch Preparation (30 minutes)

All teams prepare simultaneously. The pitch is to the "FIFA President" — played by the lead facilitator. Rules:

  • Duration: Exactly 60 seconds. A visible timer runs. The FIFA President will cut the presenter off at 60 seconds.
  • Format: Spoken word only. No slides, no props. The pitch is purely verbal.
  • Content: Teams may use their airline name, brand values, vision, target markets, route network, and any brand positioning established in Q1.
  • Order: Determined by dice roll — same mechanic as L1 Podium. Each team rolls one die. Highest goes first.
  • Audience: All 20 participants watch all 5 pitches. Teams may not leave the room between pitches.

Phase 3 — Live Pitches, Scoring & Reveal

FIFA President Scoring (50% of total)

After all 5 pitches, the FIFA President scores each privately (1–10) on:

  • Brand Fit — Does this airline feel right for the World Cup?
  • Clarity — Was the pitch crisp, memorable, specific?
  • Excitement — Did it make the room feel something?
  • Commercial Logic — Does the partnership make obvious sense?

Average of 4 dimensions = pitch score (1–10). Contributes 50% to final score.

Commercial Bid Scoring (50% of total)

After pitches, all bid envelopes opened simultaneously. Bid score calculated:

  • Highest bidder: 10 commercial points
  • All others: (their bid ÷ highest bid) × 10
  • Example: Highest bid $60M. Team bidding $30M scores 5 commercial pts.

Final score = (Pitch Score × 50%) + (Commercial Score × 50%). Highest total wins the partnership.

The fact that the audience watches all 5 pitches creates a competitive atmosphere that is hard to replicate in a standard board decision — teams that go last have seen every other airline's positioning and must adapt or commit. 🎲 Game Master Note

After all 5 pitches, take 2 minutes privately to finalise scores before the reveal. Open envelopes simultaneously (have someone assist) so bid amounts are revealed to the whole room at the same moment. Announce the winner AFTER showing the math publicly. Film the pitches if consent is given — watching them back during debrief is powerful. Debrief question: "If you had known what others were going to bid, would you have changed your pitch? What about your bid amount?"

Summary

Three years ago, your CMO signed a global entertainment celebrity as your brand ambassador — one of the most recognised faces on the planet — at $35M over two years, since renewed. Today, a 45-second video surfaced online from a private dinner two weeks ago. Someone filmed it without their knowledge. In the video, your ambassador is telling a story to friends — it's comedic, clearly relaxed, clearly candid. And the punchline of the story is a very specific, very funny, and very accurate description of a service failure on one of your flights. The bit lands perfectly. The table erupts. The video has 6.1 million views in 90 minutes. Your logo is on your ambassador's official profile. The hashtag is trending. And the worst part — they're not wrong.

What Makes This Different From Every Other Crisis

This is not a scandal. Nobody did anything wrong. Your ambassador made an accurate joke about a real experience at a private dinner. The person filming violated their privacy. The internet is simultaneously sympathetic to the ambassador AND using the joke to catalogue their own bad experiences with your airline. 14,000 passenger posts are now sharing their own version of the same story. This is not a crisis of misconduct. It is a crisis of truth — and truth is the hardest thing in PR to manage.

Your 5 Options — 30 Minutes to Decide

  • A) Terminate immediately — "This is inconsistent with our brand values" — Exit penalty: $10M. The internet's response to this statement will be immediate: "They fired someone for telling the truth." Your ambassador's fanbase, which is also your customer base, will not forget. The specific service issues they joked about remain unaddressed.
  • B) Join the joke — post your own content owning it — CMO walks into the scene from the video, says: "We heard you. You're not wrong. Here's what we're doing about it." Brave, human, self-aware. If it lands: +30 Brand Pts, viral positive moment. If it feels calculated or the presenter doesn't pull it off: -20 Brand Pts. This is the highest-risk, highest-reward option in the scenario.
  • C) Issue a formal customer apology — address the service issues, say nothing about the ambassador — Take accountability for the underlying problem. Separate the viral video from the operational improvement. Brand signal: "We heard our customers." Ambassador relationship technically continues, awkwardly.
  • D) Contact the ambassador privately — co-create a "We're Fixing It" campaign — They document your airline's visible, specific improvements over the next 2 rounds. Redemption arc content. Cost: $8M additional. Takes 2 rounds to produce. If improvements materialise: +40 Brand Pts long-term, +$22M earned media value. If improvements feel staged: "PR arc" backlash -25 Brand Pts.
  • E) Say absolutely nothing — wait for the news cycle to move on — 70% of the time: it does, within a week. 30% of the time: a journalist runs "Airline silent as ambassador exposes service failures" for 3 days. Silence is also a message — and 14,000 customer posts are waiting to see if you respond.

The Hidden Variables

Customer Loyalty matters here more than anywhere else. Teams with high customer loyalty (>70%) can choose Option B and have it land — their customers trust them enough to believe the self-deprecation is genuine. Teams with low loyalty (<45%) who try Option B will be seen as desperate. The same joke told from different brand positions lands completely differently.

Option A's cost at Q18 is not $10M — it's $10M plus the customer sentiment shift. At this stage in the game, your customers have flown with you for 5 years. Firing their favourite celebrity for telling a true story is a different decision than it would have been in Q3.

What is the specific service failure? Teams must decide this in the room before choosing a response — because the right response depends on whether the failure is fixable, past, or ongoing. If it's ongoing, Option C rings hollow. If it's already been fixed, Option B has evidence behind it.

🎭 Plot Twist — Revealed 30 Minutes Later · Game Master Eyes Only

The 14,000 passenger posts about the same service failure are now the bigger story than the video itself. A major travel publication runs: "Passengers confirm: [Ambassador] wasn't exaggerating." The specific service failure is now fully validated by public evidence. Teams that ignored it (E) now look arrogant. Teams that terminated the ambassador (A) look like they tried to silence a whistleblower — the framing shifts dramatically. Teams that issued a real apology (C) look prescient. Teams that ran the joke with the ambassador (B) are now positioned as the only airline that responded with humanity. The cost of rebuilding a depleted workforce — recruiting, retraining, recertifying pilots and crew — routinely exceeded the savings made during the downturn. The pattern is well-documented and repeats across cycles.

Summary

Leading economists and your own financial advisers are aligned: a significant recession is coming. Forecasts project passenger demand will drop 25–30% for at least 4 consecutive quarters. Your CFO says the airline cannot sustain current staffing levels through a 4-quarter downturn without burning your cash reserves to zero. The "responsible" financial decision is to cut 30% of your workforce now — approximately 4,500 people — saving $120M per year. Your CHRO believes there are alternatives. Note: if your team holds the Government Board Card from S5, mass redundancies are partially or fully blocked.

Your 4 Options

  • B) Temporary measures — pay cuts, reduced hours, voluntary leave — Save $60M/yr. All staff retained. Morale lower but recoverable. If recession extends beyond 2 quarters: may still require A.
  • C) Hold headcount — cut costs elsewhere (routes, marketing, capex) — Save $40M elsewhere. All staff retained. Most expensive near-term. Best positioned if recovery arrives quickly.
  • D) Invest counter-cyclically — hire cheap talent and acquire assets at recession prices — Burn cash now, emerge stronger if recovery comes. Highest risk. Highest potential competitive reward.

What Makes This Hard

The CFO's case for A is financially sound if the 4-quarter forecast is correct. But history shows that airlines that made mass layoffs during downturns were consistently unable to hire pilots fast enough when demand recovered earlier than their models predicted — and the rehire costs exceeded the savings. The CHRO sees this pattern. The CFO doesn't, or discounts it. The CEO must decide under real financial pressure whether to trust an economist's forecast or the CHRO's institutional memory. Option D requires strong conviction and cash reserves — most teams won't have both.

"The Moscow Signal" — Pandemic or Panic?

Q5 · Late Spring · Summer Season Starts Next Quarter · Lock-In Commitment Mechanic
ExternalLock-In

⏰ Timing Context — Critical for Game Master

This scenario plays at the end of spring. Summer tourism season — your highest-revenue quarter — begins next round (Q6). Teams are deciding whether to cut operations right as the year's most profitable period is about to begin. This timing is deliberate. The stakes are highest precisely because of what comes next.

Summary

A fast-spreading respiratory illness has been detected among workers at a freight logistics hub in Moscow, Russia. Over 18 days it has spread to 14 countries, predominantly through business travel networks. The WHO has issued an "Observation Level 2" alert — active monitoring, not yet a formal health emergency. Media coverage is intense and social media is projecting worst-case scenarios. Your airline operates 6 routes through Russian airspace and connects 4 Eastern European cities. Two of your cabin crew members who worked the Moscow route recently have reported mild flu-like symptoms. Your medical team cannot yet confirm if these are related. Summer season — your busiest quarter — starts next round. You have 30 minutes to decide your response.

⚠ Two-Part Decision — Teams Must Decide Both Simultaneously

Part 1: Choose your operational response level (how much you cut). Part 2: Declare how many quarters you will commit to that response. Both decisions are binding. A government regulation taking effect at the start of next quarter will lock any committed plan for its declared duration — you cannot reduce further, and you cannot reverse cuts early.

Part 1 — Your Operational Response

  • A) Aggressive defensive posture — Suspend all 6 Russian routes and 4 Eastern European routes. Reduce crew headcount by 20% (combination of redundancies and mandatory unpaid leave). Cut marketing budget 50%. Estimated cost savings: $28M per quarter committed.
  • B) Moderate response — Reduce flight frequency 40% on affected routes only. No staff redundancies — voluntary paid stand-down for Moscow-route crew (1 week). Reduce marketing 25%. Estimated savings: $14M per quarter committed.
  • C) Minimal response — protocols only — All routes continue normally. Enhanced health screening at boarding. No staff changes. No marketing changes. No lock-in required. Cost: +$1.5M/quarter for enhanced protocols.
  • D) Counter-position — Keep all routes and staff. Increase marketing spend by 20% targeting passengers of airlines that are pulling back. Position as "the airline still flying when others ran." No lock-in required. Cost: +$8M this quarter in additional marketing.

Part 2 — Your Commitment Period (Options A or B only)

If choosing A or B, teams must declare how many quarters they commit to that plan. This is the number written on your decision card and handed to the Game Master:

  • 1 quarter — Committed for Q5 only. Free to restore operations at Q6 start. Maximum flexibility.
  • 2 quarters — Committed through Q5 and Q6. Cannot restore until Q7 begins.
  • 3 quarters — Committed through Q5, Q6, and Q7. Cannot restore until Q8 begins.
  • 4+ quarters — Committed through Q5–Q8 (or longer). Deepest cuts, deepest lock-in.

Teams choosing C or D have no commitment period — they made no cuts, so there is nothing to lock in. They retain full operational flexibility from Q6 onward.

What Makes This Hard

The illness looks serious enough to act on — social media is amplifying fear, some crew are symptomatic, and 14 countries are affected. But acting aggressively means voluntarily cutting routes right as summer begins, the one quarter where your airline makes the most money. The commitment period mechanic adds a second layer of difficulty: locking in for fewer quarters costs more per quarter but preserves flexibility. Locking in for longer saves more money per quarter but bets the entire summer on the pandemic being real and lasting. Option D (counter-programming) is the highest risk position — maximum reward if the illness fizzles, maximum damage if it doesn't. Option C (protocols only) is the invisible option that teams often overlook: it costs almost nothing and preserves everything. The fear of looking "irresponsible" in the room often pushes teams away from C even when C may be correct.

🎭 Plot Twist — Revealed at Q6 Start · Game Master Eyes Only

At the start of Q6 (which is the beginning of summer season), the WHO releases its formal investigation findings: the Moscow illness is classified as RMSC-7, a novel strain of rhinovirus — the common cold family. It is genuinely highly contagious, but causes no serious illness in healthy individuals. No hospitalisations recorded in otherwise healthy adults. No fatalities. The panic was media-manufactured. All travel advisories are immediately lifted. Tourism boards announce summer is fully open. Passenger demand surges to its highest levels of the year.

Now reveal to each team exactly how many quarters they locked in — and what that means for their ability to meet surging demand.

⚙ Operational Lock-In — What It Means in Practice

Teams locked in cannot immediately reverse their committed decisions when the false alarm is revealed at Q6. The practical reasons:

  • Staff: Employees made redundant have already left. Rehiring and retraining takes 4–6 weeks minimum. You cannot operate at full capacity in Q6 even if you want to.

  • Aircraft: Planes taken out of service require maintenance recertification before returning to operations — this takes 2–3 weeks per aircraft.
  • Supplier contracts: Ground handling, catering, and fuel contracts on reduced routes were formally terminated with notice periods. Reinstating them takes time.

For each quarter of lock-in beyond Q5: the team operates at their reduced level regardless of market conditions. They watch competitors take their passengers. The financial and brand impact above reflects this reality.

Starting Hub Airport — Select Your Home Base

Teams choose 1 hub airport. No two teams may share the same hub. If two teams select the same airport, a blind bid is held — highest bid wins the airport, loser must choose again. The bid amount is paid immediately from cash reserves. Hub airports grant 30 permanent slots included in Q1. All other airport slots must be bid for as they release each game year.

How Airport Costs Work

Terminal Fee: Paid every quarter regardless of how many flights you operate — this is your base cost of being at that airport. Landing Fee: Paid per flight movement (one landing = one movement). Demand Multiplier: Applied to all passenger revenue generated from this hub — higher demand airports generate more revenue per route. Slot Status: Restricted airports cap your maximum flights per quarter. Route landing fees at your DESTINATION airport are paid separately per flight.

Fleet Menu — Aircraft Available in Year 2000

All aircraft below existed and were commercially available at the start of the simulation (~year 2000). New types unlock per the Aircraft Launch Schedule in the Timeline tab. Teams may buy or take on operating lease. First orders placed in Q2 — aircraft arrive Q3. No minimum fleet to begin (you will fly your first routes in Q3).

Buy vs. Lease

  • Buy (Cash or Debt): You own the aircraft. It appears on your balance sheet at purchase price. Depreciates at 5% per year (1.25%/quarter). Residual value floor: 10% of purchase price. You can sell it.
  • Operating Lease: You never own the aircraft. Quarterly lease payment listed below. Does not appear as debt on balance sheet — but cannot be sold, and lease terms run for 4 quarters minimum.

Fuel Consumption

Fuel cost per flight = Distance (km) × Fuel burn (L/km) × Fuel price ($/L). Fuel price per litre = (Fuel Index / 100) × $0.18/L baseline. At index 100 = $0.18/L. At spike (index 140) = $0.252/L. Upgrade to eco-engine reduces burn rate by 10% for the listed upgrade cost (one-time, permanent for that aircraft).

The Situation

The union representing your pilots and cabin crew has announced a strike in 36 hours. You are meeting their representatives right now. You have 30 minutes to reach a deal before extra time kicks in. You are in the room — whatever you say here is a commitment.

Where You Currently Stand — Workforce Facts
Pilot salaries vs. market average15% below market
Cabin crew salaries vs. market11% below market
Current cabin crew ratio2 flight attendants per cabin section
Rest period between duties10 hours minimum (industry standard: 12)
Annual bonus schemeNone currently in place
Crew meal allowance on layovers$18/day (competitor average: $35/day)
Union's Stated Demands (Received Yesterday)
  1. Salary: Bring pilots to market rate (+15%) and cabin crew to market rate (+11%) — effective immediately
  2. Cabin crew ratio: Increase from 2 to 6 flight attendants per cabin section on all aircraft
  3. Rest periods: Raise minimum to 14 hours between duties (from current 10)
  4. Layover allowance: Match competitor average — $35/day
  5. Annual bonus: Introduce a profit-sharing scheme of 5% of net profit shared across all crew
  6. Job protection: No involuntary crew redundancies for 2 years
🔒 What You Know — Do Not Reveal This
  • The cabin crew ratio ask is operationally impossible as stated. Going from 2 to 6 per section would require hiring ~1,400 new crew and adds $48M/yr to operating costs. However — moving to 3 per section is manageable at $16M/yr and meets safety regulations comfortably. The union knows 6 is an opening position.
  • Your salary ceiling is +9%. Your CFO has confirmed that anything above 9% salary increase across pilots tips your cost structure past the point the board approved for this year. You can offer up to 9% without a board escalation. Above that, you need to call an emergency board session — which delays the deal and costs you more in ongoing strike risk.
  • Rest periods: You can reach 12 hours (industry standard) easily. Reaching 13 hours would require rescheduling 8% of your routes, costing ~$6M in operational changes. You cannot agree to 14 without 6 months of operational restructuring.
  • The layover allowance is the cheapest concession available to you. Raising from $18 to $35/day costs $3.2M/yr and has outsized goodwill value with crew. Consider offering this early as a visible, human gesture — before the harder financial asks.
  • Profit-sharing is your strongest card. If the airline performs well, 5% of net profit could be significant for crew. If it doesn't, it costs you nothing. This can be offered in place of a higher salary increase — frame it as "we share our success with you."
⏱ Your Cost Clock — CEO and Your Exec Only · Never Share This Number

Every minute this negotiation runs costs your airline approximately 1% of your quarterly revenue. Calculate this from your current game state before entering the room. This covers the operational disruption, crew uncertainty, and media attention generated by each additional minute of unresolved industrial action. At minute 30, extra time begins — cost per minute triples. The union does not know this figure. Your team does not know this figure. Only you two do.

  • Salary: Your members will ratify any deal with at least 7% for pilots and 7% for cabin crew. Below 7%: a ratification vote will fail and the strike proceeds. Above 10%: a clear mandate win. Your opening was 15%/11% — walk toward 7% only in exchange for other real gains.
  • Cabin crew ratio: The 6-per-section figure is an opening position. Members will accept 3 per section as a genuine safety improvement — this is your real ask. Use 6 as your anchor, concede to 3 if you are getting strong salary movement.
  • Rest periods: Members need at least 12 hours — the industry standard. That is the floor. Anything above that is a bonus you negotiate for.
  • Layover allowance: Non-negotiable minimum of $25/day. Crew in expensive cities cannot function below this. This should be easy for the airline — use it to test how serious they are about the small stuff.
  • Profit-sharing: Good to have, not essential. Accept it as a complement to salary — never as a replacement. A good year with 5% profit share on top of 8% salary beats 10% salary alone in most years.
  • Job protection: Members want this but it is tradeable. You can drop it in exchange for meaningful salary movement.
Your Tactics
  • Lead with the human picture. Read the table data aloud if you need to. "Our pilots earn 15% less than pilots at comparable airlines. A captain with 18 years of experience takes home less than a junior pilot at our competitor." Make it real before it becomes a number negotiation.
  • The ratio is your anchor. The cabin crew ratio change from 2 to 6 sounds operationally extreme — and it is. That is the point. It gives you enormous room to "concede" to 3, which is actually your goal, while making the airline feel they won something significant.
  • Silence works. After you state a demand, stop talking. Let them fill the silence. They will often volunteer a constraint before you have to find it.
  • If they offer profit-sharing in place of salary: respond with "Our members work the same hours whether the airline is profitable or not. Their rent doesn't disappear in a bad quarter." Then pause.

The SPCA membership voted on how long they will allow negotiations to continue before declaring them failed and proceeding to immediate strike action. Your dead-stop minute will be written on a separate slip of paper handed to you privately by the facilitator before the session. When that minute arrives, you say: "We've given this negotiation the time our members agreed to. We don't have a mandate to continue. Good luck." Stand. Leave. Say nothing more.

🏛 What Happens After Dead-Stop — Union Advantage You Know, They Don't

When the dead-stop triggers, the airline suffers all financial and brand damage from the negotiation period — and none of the union's demands are implemented. However, the government steps in with a relief package: 50% of the total negotiation damage is refunded to the airline as an industrial dispute subsidy. The airline will not know this is coming. The effect is: the no-deal outcome is less catastrophic for the airline than they expect when the walk-out happens. From the union's perspective, this means a dead-stop is strategically effective — the airline is hurt, members get sympathy, and the government's bailout signals that the union's grievances were legitimate enough for state intervention. Do not share or hint at this. Let the corporate team discover it after the session.

The airline team does not know when this is. It is different for each pair. Do not hint at it, reference it, or use it as a threat — that would invalidate the exercise. Simply execute it when the time comes.

The Rules

  • The clock starts the moment the facilitator finishes reading the scenario

What the Facilitator (FIFA President) Watches For

  • Do they introduce themselves or assume they're remembered? — Most people assume. The best ones check.
  • Do they make it about FIFA or about their airline? — A pitch that starts with "we're great at..." rarely lands. One that starts with "I've been thinking about what the World Cup needs..." is different.
  • Do they listen? — The FIFA President will react to whatever is said. A CMO who speaks for 60 seconds non-stop is different from one who creates a conversation.
  • Do they use the clue? — Teams that noticed the Q3 news item about the FIFA President's habits may use it. Teams that didn't will pitch generically.
  • What do they ask for? — The 60 seconds almost never ends with a signed deal. The best outcome is a follow-up. Does the CMO know what they want to happen next?

🚨 CASE 1 — The Flight in the Zone

At 14:32 local time, your Operations Centre received an urgent bulletin from the civil aviation authority: a region your Flight 741 is currently traversing has been formally declared a no-fly zone due to rapidly escalating political and military activity in the area. The aircraft — a fully loaded widebody with 287 passengers and 14 crew — is 58 minutes from its destination airport and 61 minutes from being able to turn back to the origin airport. Both the forward path and the return path cross through the no-fly zone. There is no third option. The plane is in the air and the crew is awaiting your instruction.

Proceed to Destination

  • Flight time in no-fly zone: approx. 42 minutes
  • Passengers reach their destination. Minimal disruption. No refunds.
  • Risk: you are in an active no-fly zone. If an incident occurs at any point: full aircraft loss at $180M + liability to 301 passengers + families. Regulatory investigation guaranteed.
  • You are 58 minutes away from landing. The zone was declared while airborne — this was not negligence at departure.

Turn Back to Origin

  • Flight time back through no-fly zone: approx. 44 minutes
  • Passengers returned to origin. Full compensation required: $1,400 average per passenger (rebooking + hotel + meals + distress payment) = approx. $400K total. Plus $680K in operational costs for the return flight.
  • Same risk in no-fly zone but passengers end up back where they started with no outcome. Brand hit: "airline turned flight around mid-journey" story.
  • No outcome is actually safer than the other. Both cross the same zone.
FACILITATOR ONLY — Game Impact

Both options cross the no-fly zone for similar duration. The correct answer from a risk-purity standpoint is proceed to destination: shorter zone exposure, passengers reach their goal, no compensation cost. "Turning back" feels safer but is not — it adds 2 minutes of exposure, costs $1.08M, and ends with passengers in the wrong city.

Teams that proceed: no immediate cost. 2% incident probability applied (both routes treated identically). Teams that turn back: -$1.08M + Brand Pts −5 (turned back story) + same 2% incident probability. If incident occurs regardless of choice: aircraft loss $180M, liability $42M, Brand Pts −40, regulatory review initiated.

📐 DEMAND & OCCUPANCY FORMULA — ENGINEERING REFERENCE
Step 1 — Route Demand
route_tourism = cityA.tourism + cityB.tourism
route_business = cityA.business + cityB.business
amplifier = min(cityA.amp, cityB.amp)
effective_tourism = route_tourism × amplifier
effective_business = route_business × amplifier
total_route_demand = effective_tourism + effective_business
Step 2 — Airline Attractiveness Score (per route)
price_score = (avg_route_price / your_price) × 100
brand_score = your_brand_pts / 2 (cap 100)
loyalty_score = your_loyalty_pct
service_score = your_service_slider / 6 × 100
attractiveness = (price × 0.45) + (brand × 0.25) + (loyalty × 0.20) + (service × 0.10)
Step 3 — Market Share & Occupancy
total_attract = sum(all airlines on this route)
your_share = your_attract / total_attract
your_daily_pax = total_route_demand × your_share
your_daily_capacity = sum(seats × frequency)
occupancy = your_daily_pax / your_daily_capacity
capped at 98% maximum
Notes
• If only 1 airline on route: occupancy = demand / capacity (cap 98%)
• If total capacity < total demand: all airlines at 98%+ — add more flights
• Demand numbers grow per World Events + growth rate
• Amplifier always uses the LOWER of the two cities
• Price sensitivity: ±10% from avg = significant share swing

Quarterly Operations

Every quarter, teams must submit four operational decisions alongside their board decision. These run the airline — the board decisions shape it. All submissions are made directly in the simulation platform before the next quarter begins.

✈️ 1 — Plane Procurement Order

Submit a purchase order for aircraft from the current market. Planes arrive the following quarter and can be deployed from that point. Orders must specify aircraft type, quantity, and financing method (cash or debt).

FieldInput
Aircraft typeSelect from available fleet (see Aircraft Specs tab)
Quantity1 to max available in pool
FinancingCash (immediate) or Debt (at current quarter rate)
Delivery quarterAlways: next quarter (Q+1)

🎟 0 — Slot Bidding (When Available)

Each airport releases new slots once per game year at a random quarter (announced 1 quarter ahead). Teams bid sealed amounts — highest bidders win. Slots are permanent once purchased.

TierBase Price/SlotStart InventoryQ20 Target
Tier 1 (LHR, JFK, DXB etc.)$120K/slot200 slots~1,000

Bid submission is part of the quarterly ops form when a slot auction is active. Hub airports: 30 permanent slots granted at Q1. Unused released slots accumulate — next release batch adds to the pool. Slots at both endpoints are required for each weekly flight. 1 slot = 1 weekly flight.

🗺️ 2 — Route Management

Open new routes, close existing ones, or adjust frequency. Route changes take effect next quarter. Opening a route at a new airport requires paying that airport's slot fee per daily departure.

ActionCost Structure
Open new routeSlot fee × daily departures × 91 days/quarter per new airport
Close a routeSlot fee waived — but slots may be reclaimed by airport authority
Increase frequencyEach additional daily departure: +1× slot fee per day
Hub airportTerminal fee paid quarterly regardless of volume (see Airport Menu)
